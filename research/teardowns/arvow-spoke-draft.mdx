Arvow grew to roughly $70K in monthly recurring revenue, about $1M a year, and its founder says it was grown purely with YouTube, but not on the company's brand channel. The growth engine was founder Vasco Monteiro publishing daily buyer-intent tutorials on his own personal SEO channels. This is a teardown of exactly how he did it, sourced from his own interviews and linked inline. It is one of six in our [B2B YouTube case-study collection](/blog/youtube-marketing-case-studies).

The obvious play is to spin up a branded company channel and post a launch video. Arvow's founder did the opposite, and it is the whole lesson.

**Arvow, an AI SEO article writer, reached roughly $70K in monthly recurring revenue, about $1M a year, across around 600 paying clients, and its founder says it was grown purely with YouTube. The engine was not the company's brand channel. It was founder Vasco Monteiro on his own personal SEO channels, publishing a 20 to 30 minute buyer-intent tutorial every single day for almost a year. He built video assets in three buckets, evergreen how-to content, news-jacking clips on Google algorithm updates, and viral-for-software explainers, then multiplied himself by spinning up more channels and hiring camera-comfortable creators. He tracked every signup back to a specific video with coupon codes and a which-creator-sent-you quiz, and moved his call to action to the start and middle of each video, which lifted signups. The takeaway for B2B: a founder on camera answering literal search queries can carry an entire acquisition channel.**

## The Founder Behind Arvow

Arvow is an AI SEO article writer, and its ICP is founders and marketers doing their own SEO and content. The company is run by CEO Vasco Monteiro alongside co-founder Afonso, and Vasco is the face of its acquisition.

He did not start here. His background runs from rank-and-rent SEO, into freelancing on Fiverr and Upwork, through building and selling a services marketplace called Vetted, and then into Arvow. That history matters because it explains the strategy: he already knew that search intent, not reach, is what converts, and he applied that instinct to video.

The revenue is founder-stated. In his interview Vasco reports roughly $70K in monthly recurring revenue across around 600 paying clients on plans from $99 to $700 a month, "basically a million dollars a year." The <a href="https://www.starterstory.com/vasco" rel="nofollow">Starter Story writeup</a> summarizes the figure as $79K a month. Both point at about $1M a year, and we use his spoken number.

## The Strategy: Three Buckets, Then Multiply

Vasco does not treat YouTube as a content calendar. He treats it as an asset factory, and he sorts every video into one of three buckets.

The first bucket is evergreen how-to content, videos like "best AI SEO writer" that answer a buyer-intent query and keep ranking for years. The second is news-jacking: fast videos on Google algorithm updates that spike traffic and pull in subscribers. The third is viral-for-software explainers built to travel further than a pure tutorial.

Here is where it gets interesting. Instead of scaling one channel, he multiplied himself. He spun up several personal channels, Vasco's SEO Tips, Tim, SEO Guru, and SEO News, and hired camera-comfortable creators off Upwork paid per performance, then expanded into Portuguese and Spanish. The brand channel, @ArvowAI, exists, but the audience and the signups came from these founder-led personal channels.

The volume behind it is the part most teams will not copy. In his own words: "I did 20 to 30 minute videos every single day for almost a year." The kit was deliberately plain, Loom in HD, a Miro whiteboard, Camtasia, and a Shure SM7B, "just me in my basement."

Two operational choices made the traffic measurable. He tracked signups per video with a coupon code and a message telling users to tell support that Vasco sent them, plus a signup quiz asking which creator sent you. And he moved the call to action to the start and middle of each video rather than the end, which produced a real signup lift.

The clearest proof is a single video. His tutorial on how to make a Wikipedia page, about two years old, has pulled 1.2M impressions and draws roughly 300 views a day, with 65% of views coming from YouTube search and another 20% from Google search, and it now shows up inside AI answers. One evergreen buyer-intent video, ranking on two search engines and an LLM at once.

## Steal This Playbook

The concrete steps a B2B team can lift straight from Arvow:

1. Put a real person on camera, ideally the founder, and publish buyer-intent tutorials rather than polishing a brand account first.
2. Sort every planned video into one of the three buckets, and build a base of evergreen how-to content before chasing anything trend-based.
3. Move your call to action to the first third of the video, and make it specific rather than a generic "link in the description."
4. Add per-video attribution now: a coupon code, a "how did you hear about us" field, or a which-creator-sent-you quiz at signup.
5. Once a video ranks and then stalls organically, consider in-feed YouTube ads to push a proven winner further, not an untested one.

## FAQ

### Did Arvow really grow on YouTube alone?

By the founder's own account, yes. Vasco Monteiro says on his companion video that most of Arvow's users come from YouTube and that the business was grown purely with YouTube, and his own <a href="https://x.com/vascoabm/status/1932762748472983923" rel="nofollow">tweet</a> corroborates it. The company reached roughly $70K in monthly recurring revenue, about $1M a year, across around 600 paying clients. Treat it as founder-stated attribution, not an audited breakdown, but the claim is direct and on the record. Our [YouTube ROI guide for SaaS](/blog/youtube-roi-for-saas) covers how to value that kind of channel.

### Should I use my brand channel or a personal channel for this?

Arvow owns a brand channel, but its growth came from the founder's personal SEO channels, not the company account. Vasco published as himself across several personal channels and hired camera-comfortable creators to do the same. If I had to pick one to start, I would put a real person on camera before polishing a brand account, because buyers follow people who answer their question, not logos.

### How often did Arvow's founder actually post?

Vasco says he did 20 to 30 minute videos every single day for almost a year. That cadence, held across personal channels rather than one brand account, is the part most B2B teams underestimate. He filmed with a simple kit, Loom, a Miro whiteboard, Camtasia, and a Shure SM7B microphone, and described it as just him in his basement. The output mattered more than the production budget.

### How do I track which videos actually bring signups?

Copy Arvow's tracking stack. Vasco used a per-video coupon code, a message asking new users to tell support that he sent them, and a signup quiz that asks which creator sent you. That gives per-video attribution without guesswork. He also moved his call to action to the start and middle of each video instead of the end, which lifted signups, so make the ask early and make it specific.
