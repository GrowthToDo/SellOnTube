Six B2B companies here acquire customers from YouTube by doing the same three things, and none of them do it by going viral. Every strategy guide preaches YouTube for B2B. This post is six companies that actually did it, with the receipts pulled from founder interviews and linked inline. The channels most B2B advice tells you to follow, SaaStr, Dan Martell, are educators building audiences. The six below are companies quietly using YouTube as an acquisition engine, and their numbers come from the founders' own mouths.

Treat YouTube as a "views" channel and you build an audience that never buys. Treat it as a search engine for buyers and a 370-view video can pay your rent.

**Every company in this teardown runs the same three-part play. First, they make buyer-intent video that answers a literal search query a prospect types, "how do I upload a CV," "how to split a design in Photoshop," "how to share a PDF as a link," not broad topic content built for reach. Second, they accept low view counts in exchange for high-intent signups: Bulk Mockup turned a 370-view tutorial into three paying customers, and Scalelist credits its small channel with half of all paid users. Third, they optimize each video to rank on two search engines at once, YouTube and Google, and increasingly inside AI answers, so one asset compounds instead of expiring. This is B2B YouTube treated as a YouTube acquisition channel, not a content platform. The metric that matters is customer acquisition, not subscribers or views.**

## Does YouTube Actually Work for B2B SaaS?

Yes. YouTube works for B2B SaaS when it is run as a customer acquisition channel rather than a reach channel. The six companies in this analysis prove it with founder-sourced numbers, not theory. Scalelist's founder credits YouTube with 50% of the company's paid users from a channel of roughly 2,660 subscribers. Arvow's founder says his roughly $1M-a-year business was grown purely with YouTube. Bulk Mockup turned a single 370-view tutorial into three paying customers. None of them went viral, and none of them chased subscribers. What connects all six is a buyer-intent video strategy: each publishes videos that answer the literal question a prospect searches when they have a problem the product solves, then optimizes that video to rank on both YouTube and Google. Reach is optional, intent is the point.

## Arvow: Founder-Led YouTube at Scale

*How did an AI SEO tool reach roughly $1M a year purely through YouTube?*

<div style="background: #f8fafc; border: 1px solid #e2e8f0; border-radius: 8px; padding: 1rem 1.25rem; margin: 1.25rem 0;">

**Snapshot.** Arvow is an AI SEO article writer, and founder Vasco Monteiro reports it running at roughly $70K in monthly recurring revenue, about $1M a year, across around 600 paying clients on $99 to $700 per month plans. He states it plainly in his <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VZ1XspToV1E" rel="nofollow">companion video</a>: "This business was grown purely with YouTube." His <a href="https://x.com/vascoabm/status/1932762748472983923" rel="nofollow">own tweet</a> corroborates it. The <a href="https://www.starterstory.com/vasco" rel="nofollow">Starter Story writeup</a> summarizes the number as $79K per month. Both figures land at about $1M a year.

**The channel strategy.** Arvow owns a brand channel, but the growth engine was the founder's personal SEO channels, not the company account. Vasco built money-making assets in three buckets: evergreen tutorials ("best AI SEO writer"), news-jacking videos on Google algorithm updates for subscriber spikes, and viral-for-software explainers. He then multiplied himself, spinning up several channels and hiring camera-comfortable creators paid per performance, and expanding into Portuguese and Spanish.

**The one thing others do not.** He treated volume as a personal discipline. "I did 20 to 30 minute videos every single day for almost a year." That cadence, run across founder-led personal channels rather than a polished brand account, is what most B2B teams will not sustain.

**Evidence it works.** His proof video, a two-year-old tutorial on how to make a Wikipedia page, has 1.2M impressions and pulls about 300 views a day, with 65% of views from YouTube search and another 20% from Google search, and it now surfaces inside AI answers. He also moved his call to action to the start and middle of videos rather than the end, which lifted signups.

<YouTubeFacade id="VZ1XspToV1E" title="How Arvow was grown purely with YouTube (Vasco Monteiro)" caption="Arvow founder Vasco Monteiro's companion video, the source for the 'grown purely with YouTube' claim and the ~$70K/mo MRR figure cited above." />

**Audit score: 60 of 100** (as of July 2026): Titles 20, Descriptions 10, Consistency 18, SEO 12. The current personal channel scores mid-pack, strong on buyer-intent titles but thin on descriptions and on-page SEO. A useful reminder that the score grades today's uploads, not the growth-era run that built a $1M business.

**Steal this.** Put the founder on camera and publish a daily buyer-intent tutorial for a defined sprint, not the brand account. Build a small library of evergreen how-to videos before you touch anything trend-based. Move your call to action to the first third of the video.

**Snapshot.** Tiiny Host is a web and PDF hosting SaaS built by solo founder Elston Baretto, a former JP Morgan engineer who launched it in January 2020. On <a href="https://www.starterstory.com/stories/tiiny-host" rel="nofollow">Starter Story</a> he reports about $15k in monthly recurring revenue, roughly $180k a year, with around 100,000 monthly visitors and about 1,000 paying subscribers. A later <a href="https://www.profitablefounder.xyz/blog/how-elston-built-tiny-host-to-1m-a-year" rel="nofollow">Profitable Founder interview</a> frames it as a $1M-a-year business, which we attribute to that interview rather than treat as an established figure.

**The channel strategy.** Faceless tutorials, no personal brand, each one aimed at a low-competition buyer-intent query. The titles are the strategy: "How to Share a PDF as a Link," "How to Upload Content to Your Own Web Address," "The Easiest Way to Embed PDFs on a Website." Every one is a literal job someone is trying to get done right now.

**The one thing others do not.** Baretto refuses to target the product category. On <a href="https://indiebites.com/80/transcript" rel="nofollow">Indie Bites</a> he sums it up: nobody searches "web hosting," they search "how do I upload a CV." He captures the task, and the product is the answer.

**Evidence it works.** His <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mo8kKZIMNhg" rel="nofollow">"How to Share a PDF as a Link" video</a> ranks as the top video result in Google by his account, with 30k+ views, and PDF hosting makes up roughly 40% of revenue. A tutorial that answers one search question funnels a whole product line.

**Audit score: 81 of 100** (as of July 2026): Titles 21, Descriptions 18, Consistency 20, SEO 22. The highest SEO score in the set, which is exactly what you would expect from a channel engineered to rank tutorials inside Google.

**Steal this.** List the ten literal tasks your product completes and write one video title per task, phrased exactly as a customer would type it. Skip your category keyword entirely. Let the search demand you already know exists find the answer you publish.

## Bulk Mockup: A Flywheel Built on Customer Pain

*Can a Photoshop plugin get paying customers from low-view YouTube tutorials?*

<div style="font-weight: 700; text-transform: uppercase; letter-spacing: 0.05em; font-size: 0.72rem; color: #64748b; align-self: center;">The one move</div><div style="color: #334155;">Turn logged customer pain into a tutorial for each real problem</div>

**Snapshot.** Bulk Mockup is a Photoshop plugin that batch-replaces smart objects to mass-produce mockups, built by founder Vikash Kumar Prajapati and reported at $12K per month on the <a href="https://open.spotify.com/episode/3OKeKCazbAFPOycLLgjMJ9" rel="nofollow">Starter Story episode</a> "How I Grew My Plugin to $12K/Month." His "split the design" tutorial drew 370 views in four months and produced three customers at $15 a month, about $45 in new MRR from a video almost nobody watched. A second video pulled 12,000 views in six months. And 22% of the channel's views come from Google search, so the same asset ranks in two places.

Optimize the title, description, and first 30 seconds of the transcript for that phrase so the video ranks on Google too. Judge each video on customers, not views.

## FAQ

### Does YouTube actually work for B2B?

Yes, when you run it for acquisition instead of reach. Scalelist's founder says YouTube brings 50% of the company's paid users, and Arvow's founder says the business was grown purely with YouTube. Both are small channels. The pattern across all six case studies here is the same: buyer-intent videos that answer a literal search question convert far better than high-view content built to grow an audience.

### How many views do you need to make $10,000 a month on YouTube?

For a B2B company, that is the wrong question. It assumes you earn from ad revenue, where views equal money. B2B YouTube earns from customer acquisition, where a single buyer can be worth thousands. Bulk Mockup's founder reported a 370-view video that produced three paying customers. A handful of the right viewers beats tens of thousands of the wrong ones, so measure signups and pipeline, not views. Our [YouTube ROI guide for SaaS](/blog/youtube-roi-for-saas) walks through the math.

### How long until YouTube drives leads for a B2B company?

Plan for months, not days. Scalelist's founder says YouTube started picking up after a few months before it became the company's largest acquisition channel. LocalRank's founder spent time posting daily to build an audience, then launched with revenue already flowing. YouTube compounds slowly because each video keeps ranking, so the return arrives after a body of buyer-intent content exists, not after your first upload.

### Do I need a lot of views to get customers from YouTube?

No. Low view counts convert well when the views are high intent. Bulk Mockup's founder describes a video with 370 views over four months that produced three paying customers, and LocalRank's founder says low-view videos drive more product signups than posts with tens of thousands of impressions elsewhere. In B2B, 300 views from buyers beats 30,000 views from spectators.

### Can one YouTube video rank on Google as well?

Yes, and that is a large part of why this works. Bulk Mockup's founder says 22% of the channel's views come from Google search. Arvow's founder describes a single tutorial that pulls 65% of its views from YouTube search and another 20% from Google, and is now surfaced inside AI answers too. One optimized video can rank on two search engines at once, so the asset compounds instead of expiring.

### What kind of videos should a B2B company make on YouTube?

Make buyer-intent videos that answer the literal question a prospect types when they have a problem your product solves. Tiiny Host's founder puts it plainly: nobody searches "web hosting," they search "how do I upload a CV." Target the low-competition how-to query, solve it completely on camera, and let the person who needed that answer discover your product. That is what separates a B2B YouTube strategy from a creator one.
