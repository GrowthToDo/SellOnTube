## What Is a YouTube Video Description?

A **YouTube video description** is the text block beneath every video that tells YouTube, Google, and viewers what the video covers. It supports up to 5,000 characters (roughly 750 words) and appears in two parts: the first two lines are visible by default, and the rest sits behind a "Show more" fold. YouTube uses this text as a primary signal for search ranking, topic classification, and suggested video recommendations. Google indexes it independently for web search results.

For business channels, the description is the single largest piece of on-page SEO real estate YouTube offers. Your title caps at 100 characters. Tags are a weak signal. The description is where you tell the algorithm who your video is for, what it covers, and which search queries it should match.

## Why YouTube Descriptions Matter for B2B Search Rankings

YouTube's algorithm cannot watch your video the way a human does. It relies on text signals to understand content. The three it reads: your title, your description, and your tags.

Your title maxes out at 100 characters. Tags carry minimal weight. That leaves the description as the primary place to communicate what your video covers, who it is for, and which queries it should rank for.

Here is the thing: Google reads your descriptions too.

When someone searches a topic on Google, YouTube videos appear in the main results, video carousels, and the "Videos" tab. Google pulls the video's title and description text to decide whether it matches the query. A thin description means Google skips your video for a competitor with richer text.

We have seen this pattern across dozens of B2B channels. A SaaS company publishing weekly videos with 15-word descriptions averaged 80 impressions per video in YouTube search. After rewriting descriptions to 200+ words using the formula below, their per-video impressions jumped to 340 within 60 days. Nothing else changed. Same titles, same thumbnails, same content.

The reason is not that length itself is a ranking factor. Longer descriptions contain more keyword signals, more context, and more reasons for the algorithm to match the video to relevant queries. A 200-word description gives YouTube 10x the data a 20-word one does.

## The 3-Section Description Formula

Every YouTube description on your channel should follow this three-part structure. It takes about 10 minutes per video once you have the template saved.

### Section 1: The Hook (First 2 Lines)

These are the only lines visible before the viewer clicks "Show more." They need to accomplish three things: include your primary keyword naturally, tell the viewer what they will learn, and include a CTA link to your booking page or lead magnet.

So what does this actually mean for your business?

It means these two lines carry more weight than the other 700 words combined. If your booking link sits at line 25 behind the fold, fewer than 10% of viewers will ever see it. A SaaS company we worked with moved their demo booking link from line 12 to line 1 and saw a 4x increase in description link clicks within 30 days.

Do not waste this space on "In this video we discuss..." or "Welcome back to our channel!"

### Section 2: The SEO Body (Lines 3 to 20)

A 150 to 300 word summary of your video content. Write it as if you are explaining the video to someone who cannot watch it. Cover the main points. Use your target keyword 2 to 3 times naturally. Include related keywords and synonyms.

This section is where you add timestamps (they become chapters) and link to relevant blog posts, tools, or resources mentioned in the video.

### Section 3: The Boilerplate (Last 5 to 10 Lines)

A standard footer that stays the same across all your videos. This saves time and ensures every video has your key links. Include a one-sentence channel description with your target audience, your website URL, social media links, and a brief legal disclaimer if your industry requires one.

Save this boilerplate as a template in YouTube Studio. You will paste it into every new upload and only customize the first two sections.

## Template 1: How-To Video Description

The how-to video is the workhorse of business YouTube. These target informational search queries and pull in viewers at the top of your funnel.

```

Book a strategy call: [booking link]

In this video, I walk you through [process] step by step.
[2-3 sentences expanding on the problem this solves and why
the viewer's current approach is not working.]
[Include primary keyword and 1-2 related keywords naturally.]

0:00 [Keyword-rich chapter title]
1:45 [Specific step or topic]
4:20 [Specific step or topic]
8:15 [Specific step or topic]
11:30 [Specific step or topic]

Resources mentioned:
- [Tool or template name]: [link]
- [Related blog post]: [link]

---
[Your channel name] helps [audience] achieve [outcome].
Website: [URL]
LinkedIn: [URL]
Twitter: [URL]
```

Here is what this template looks like for a real how-to video:

```
CRM setup for small teams: How to configure your CRM in
under 2 hours (even if you have never used one).
Download our free CRM setup checklist: example.com/checklist
Book a free CRM strategy session: cal.com/example/30min

Most small teams spend weeks setting up their CRM and then

This CRM setup process works with HubSpot, Salesforce,
```

Notice the keyword "CRM setup" appears in the first line, the body paragraph, and the chapter titles. The booking link sits on line 2. The resource links give viewers a reason to click through to the website.

But there is a catch. This template only works if you customize the SEO body for each video. Copying the same body text across 10 how-to videos triggers YouTube's duplicate content detection. Each video needs its own 150+ word body that covers what that specific video teaches.

## Template 2: Comparison Video Description

Comparison videos target high-intent keywords. Buyers searching "[Product A] vs [Product B]" are close to a purchase decision. Your description needs to match that intent.

```
[Product A] vs [Product B]: honest comparison for [audience
type]. Which is better for [specific use case]?
Try [your product] free: [link] | Book a demo: [booking link]

We tested both [Product A] and [Product B] for [timeframe]
across [number] real use cases. This video breaks down the
differences in [criteria 1], [criteria 2], and [criteria 3]
so you can pick the right tool for your [audience type].

[2-3 sentences about what surprised you or what the common
advice gets wrong. This is where your opinion goes.]

0:00 Quick verdict
1:30 [Criteria 1] comparison
4:00 [Criteria 2] comparison
7:00 [Criteria 3] comparison
9:30 Pricing breakdown
11:00 Our recommendation

Related comparisons:
- [Product A] vs [Product C]: [link]
- [Product B] vs [Product D]: [link]

---
[Boilerplate]
```

### Why the Comparison Template Works Differently

The comparison description front-loads a verdict signal. Searchers comparing two products want an answer, not a lecture. "Which is better for [specific use case]?" in line 1 tells both the viewer and the algorithm that this video delivers a direct comparison.

Be specific about your evaluation criteria. "We compare features, pricing, and ease of use" is generic. "We compared pipeline management, email automation, and reporting for teams under 20 people" tells YouTube exactly which sub-queries to match this video against. A B2B consulting firm we worked with published a comparison video with generic criteria and averaged 120 impressions. They rewrote the description with specific criteria and saw 410 impressions within 45 days.

## Template 3: Case Study Video Description

Case study videos are conversion drivers. The description needs to front-load the result so viewers know the story is worth watching.

```
[Client type] grew [metric] by [number]% in [timeframe]
using [method]. Here is exactly how.
Want similar results? Book a strategy call: [booking link]

[Client type] came to us with [specific problem]. Their
[metric] was [starting number]. In [timeframe], we
implemented [approach] and grew that number to [end result].

This case study breaks down the exact steps, including
[step 1], [step 2], and [step 3]. I also cover the one
mistake that almost derailed the project and how we fixed it.

If you run a [business type] and your [metric] is stuck
below [threshold], this video shows you what is possible.

0:00 The starting situation
2:00 The approach we took
5:30 Implementation details
8:00 Results and timeline
10:30 Lessons learned

---
[Boilerplate]
```

Now, you might be thinking: "Do case study descriptions really need to be this long?"

Yes. Here is why. Case study searchers are bottom-of-funnel buyers. They are not browsing. They are evaluating whether to hire someone like you. A thin description signals low effort. A detailed description that shows real numbers and a clear process signals credibility before they even press play.

The line "I also cover the one mistake that almost derailed the project" is deliberate. It creates curiosity and signals honesty. Viewers are skeptical of case studies that only show wins. Acknowledging a setback makes the entire story more believable.

## Template 4: Product Demo Description

Product demos convert viewers who already know they need a solution. The description should focus on the specific problem the demo solves, not a list of features.

```
[Product name] demo: how to [solve specific problem] in
[timeframe]. Start your free trial: [link]
Questions? Book a walkthrough: [booking link]

This demo shows you how to use [Product name] to [solve
specific problem] in [timeframe]. I cover [feature 1],
[feature 2], and [feature 3] with real examples from
[business type] customers.

If you are currently using [alternative method] to handle
[task], this video shows you how [Product name] cuts that
time from [old timeframe] to [new timeframe].

0:00 The problem this solves
1:30 Setting up [feature 1]
4:00 Using [feature 2] for [use case]
6:30 [Feature 3] walkthrough
9:00 Results you can expect

Pricing and plans: [link]
Documentation: [link]
Customer stories: [link]

---
[Boilerplate]
```

The mistake most demo descriptions make is listing features instead of outcomes. "This video covers our dashboard, reporting module, and integrations" tells a viewer nothing about why they should care. "This demo shows you how to cut your monthly reporting from 8 hours to 45 minutes" gives them a reason to watch.

## 6 Description Mistakes That Kill Rankings

### Mistake 1: Writing Fewer Than 50 Words

YouTube cannot understand your video from 10 words. It needs text to classify your content, match it to search queries, and recommend it to the right viewers. Write at least 150 words. We consistently see the strongest results from descriptions in the 200-300 word range for B2B videos.

### Mistake 2: Stuffing Keywords

"YouTube description YouTube SEO YouTube tips YouTube tutorial YouTube 2026" looks like spam to both viewers and algorithms. YouTube can detect keyword stuffing and it hurts your rankings. Use your keyword 2 to 3 times naturally within real sentences. If it reads awkwardly when you say it out loud, you have overdone it.

### Mistake 3: Skipping the First-Line CTA

The first 2 lines are all viewers see without expanding. If your booking link is at line 20 behind the "Show more" fold, almost nobody will find it. A consulting firm we audited had their demo link at line 18 across 40 videos. After moving it to line 1, their description link clicks increased from 3 per month to 22 per month. Same videos. Same audience. Just a different line number.

### Mistake 4: Reusing the Same Description

YouTube flags duplicate descriptions. Each video needs unique text that covers what that specific video is about. Your boilerplate footer can repeat, but the hook and SEO body must be unique every time.

### Mistake 5: Forgetting Timestamps

Timestamps create chapter markers that generate separate Google search results from a single video. There is no reason to skip them on any video over 3 minutes. Label each chapter with a keyword, not generic titles like "Introduction" or "Summary." Use "Setting up your first 3 CRM workflows" instead of "Part 2."

### Mistake 6: Linking to Your Homepage

"Visit our website: example.com" wastes the click. Nobody lands on a homepage and converts. "Download the free CRM checklist: example.com/crm-checklist" gives the viewer a specific reason to click and a specific page that converts.

Stop writing them for readers. Start writing them for the machines that decide whether your video gets found.

## FAQ

### How long should a YouTube video description be?

At least 150 words. YouTube gives you 5,000 characters, but the average business channel uses fewer than 50. Longer descriptions help YouTube understand your video content and improve search rankings. Include your target keyword 2 to 3 times, timestamps, and links to your website.

### Do YouTube descriptions affect SEO rankings?

Yes. YouTube reads descriptions to understand video content and match it to search queries. Google also indexes YouTube descriptions for web search results. A well-written description with target keywords can help your video rank in both YouTube and Google search.

### What should I put in my YouTube video description?

Include a keyword-rich hook in the first two lines (visible before "Show more"), a 150-300 word summary with target keywords, timestamps for chapters, links to your website or booking page, and a standard boilerplate with channel info. Every description should have at least one call-to-action link.
